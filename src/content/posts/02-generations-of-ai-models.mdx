  <p class="week-tag animate-in">Week 2: Generations of AI Models</p>
  <h1 class="animate-in">Three models. One problem. Three different answers.</h1>
  <p class="animate-in">Have you noticed there are two very different kinds of question we ask AI now? Sometimes you want a quick answer. Sometimes you want it to actually do something for you. Those are not the same request, and they have not always been served by the same kind of model.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Large language models have evolved through three generations over the past five years, moving from systems that could answer your questions to systems that can finish your work. Each generation added a capability the one before it did not have. Understanding which generation you are talking to is the difference between knowing why a task succeeded and being surprised by why it did not.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Take a concrete request. Plan and book a three-city trip next month under two thousand dollars. A chat model will list flights in five seconds and the math will often be wrong. A reasoning model will take half a minute, work through the budget carefully, and hand you a clean itinerary. An agentic model will take four minutes and book the flights itself, calling tools and reacting to what the world says back, without you in the middle of it.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">All three are large language models. Underneath, they share the same architecture from week 1. The difference is not in the engine. It is in everything around the engine.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">How can the same architecture produce such different behavior?</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="generation-timeline" index={1}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">Three generations.</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">The three kinds of model you just saw answering the same prompt are not just different products. They represent three distinct generations in the evolution of the technology, and each one introduced a capability the previous generation did not have.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">The first generation, the chat model, reads tokens and writes the next one. We informally call it the predictor. Most of what people meant by "AI" between 2020 and 2023 belongs here. It was the first generation that felt fluent enough to be useful.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">The second generation, the reasoning model, writes a long internal chain of thought before it answers. Call it the thinker. The first widely used examples arrived in late 2024, and within a year they were beating chat models on nearly every hard reasoning benchmark.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">The third generation, the agentic model, runs a loop. It plans, calls a tool, observes what comes back, decides whether to call another, and keeps going until the goal is met. Call it the doer. Where a chat or reasoning model answers your question and stops, an agent pursues a goal across many turns without you in the loop. It started showing up in production through 2025 and is what most people now mean when they say "an AI agent".</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Each generation did not replace the one before it. They coexist, and each one is the right choice for a different kind of work. You still want a chat model when you need an answer in a second. You want an agentic model when you need work to actually finish.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Underneath, all three share the same architecture from week 1. So what actually changed between them?</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="forward-pass" index={2}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">One forward pass.</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">
    The atomic unit of the chat model is a <Term word="Forward pass" definition="One trip through the neural network. Tokens come in, one new token comes out. The model repeats this for every word it writes." analogy="Pulling a single card from a deck. The model pulls one. Then shuffles in the result. Then pulls again.">forward pass</Term>.
  </p>
  <p class="animate-in">A chat model reads a sequence of tokens, runs them through the network once, and produces one new token at the top. It then appends that token to the sequence and runs the network again. Predict, append, predict, append. The thirteen words you typed go in, and ninety-four words come out, one at a time.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">This is what made the first generation feel magical. It is also what makes it brittle on hard problems. There is no time for the model to check its own work, no chance to back up and try a different approach. Whatever it produces in that first pass is the answer it commits to.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">A chat model is fast and often wrong on real reasoning. So what happens if you let it think longer before answering?</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="internal-cot" index={3}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">One internal thought.</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">
    The atomic unit of the reasoning model is the <Term word="Chain of thought" definition="A long internal note the model writes to itself before answering. The note never reaches the user. The final answer is informed by it." analogy="Scratch paper for a math problem. The teacher only sees the final answer. The student needed twenty lines to get there.">chain of thought</Term>.
  </p>
  <p class="animate-in">Before a reasoning model answers your question, it writes itself a long internal note. Two hundred lines is normal. Two thousand is not unusual on a hard problem. The note never reaches your screen, but it is what produces the answer.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">The model tries an approach, catches its own mistake, abandons that path, and tries another. By the time it commits to a final answer, most of the thinking has already happened. You see one paragraph. The model wrote a chapter to get there.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">This is why a reasoning model gets math right when a chat model does not. It is not a smarter network. It is the same network, given more chances to second-guess itself before it speaks.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">A reasoning model can also call tools, the same way a chat model can. Most of the recent ones do. What separates a reasoning model from an agentic one is not whether it calls tools at all. It is whether the user is still in the loop after each answer. The reasoning model finishes its chain of thought, hands you the result, and stops. You are the next move.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">A reasoning model still ends every interaction by writing words at you. What happens when a model can keep going on its own?</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="tool-call" index={4}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">One loop iteration.</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">
    The atomic unit of the agentic model is one cycle of the <Term word="ReAct loop" definition="The reason-act-observe cycle that drives an agentic model. The model reasons about what to do, takes an action via a tool call, observes the result, and reasons again. Named after the 2022 'ReAct' paper that formalized the pattern." analogy="A line cook reading the order, calling 'order in!' to the kitchen, tasting what comes back, then deciding the next step. The cook does not stop after one dish.">ReAct loop</Term>. Reason. Act. Observe. Reason again.
  </p>
  <p class="animate-in">A tool is exposed to the model the way a function is exposed to a programmer. It has a name, a one-line description of what it does, and a JSON schema that lists the arguments it takes and what each one means. You write the description. The model reads it. The model picks which tool to call by reasoning over the descriptions, the same way you pick which library function to call by reading the documentation.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">When the model decides on a call, it emits a structured request with the function name and the argument values. A runtime takes that request and executes it against the real world, hits the airline API, runs the SQL, sends the email. The result comes back to the model. The model reasons about what came back and decides what to do next, which usually means another tool call.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Three iterations becomes a paragraph. Twenty becomes a finished task. The trip-planning request from the first scene is solved by an agentic model not because it thinks harder than a reasoning model, but because it can run this loop without you in the middle of it.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Three different atomic moves at the bottom of three different generations. Now look at how each one scales.</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="neural-net-three-loops" index={5}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">The same engine, three different cars.</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">All three generations share the transformer architecture from week 1. Same attention, same feed-forward layers, same parameter counts in roughly the same order of magnitude. What changes is the loop wrapped around the network.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">A chat model runs the network once per token, appends the result, and repeats. It is the simplest possible loop, and it is what got the field started.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">A reasoning model runs the network thousands of times silently, generating an internal monologue, before showing you anything. The loop is the same shape, just much longer and hidden from view.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">An agentic model runs the network, then steps out to call a tool, then runs the network again on what came back. The loop reaches outside the model into the world and pulls a result back in.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Same engine, three different cars built around it. The architecture does not predict which kind of behavior you get. The loop does.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">If the architecture is the same, where does the new capability come from each time?</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="training-curve" index={6}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">First axis. Training compute.</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">The first generation got more capable for one reason. The labs spent more money training the network.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">
    More parameters, more data, more <Term word="Training compute" definition="The total compute spent teaching the model. Paid once, by the lab, before the model ever sees a user." analogy="The cost of building a factory. A one-time bill spread across every product the factory ever ships.">training compute</Term>. In 2018 the leading models had around a hundred million parameters. By 2020 they had crossed a hundred billion. By 2023 they had crossed a trillion. For five straight years, every order of magnitude of training compute bought a measurable jump in capability.
  </p>
  <p class="animate-in">And then the curve started to bend. The next ten times of training compute did not produce the next ten times of capability. The lab profit-and-loss statements felt it before the public did, and something else had to give.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Training the network harder kept making models smarter, until it stopped. So the field looked for a new axis to scale on.</p>
  <p class="animate-in scene-source">Parameter counts come from the original GPT and GPT-3 papers and from public reporting on later frontier models. The trillion-parameter figure is widely cited but never officially confirmed.</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="inference-curve" index={7}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">Second axis. Inference compute.</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">The second generation found the new axis. Instead of making the model smarter at training time, the labs let it think longer at the moment it answers your question.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">
    The first widely used reasoning model spent thirty seconds reasoning silently before producing an answer. The improvement curve was log-linear, meaning every doubling of <Term word="Inference compute" definition="The compute spent every time the model answers a question. Paid by you, every query." analogy="The electricity bill of running the factory. Pay it every shift, for every product.">inference compute</Term> per query unlocked roughly the next tier of difficulty. Hard math problems became solvable. So did multi-step code edits and contract review.
  </p>
  <p class="animate-in">Training compute is a one-time cost the lab pays before the model ever sees a customer. Inference compute is a cost you pay, every single query. The new generation moved a large piece of the bill onto the user, and it is part of why a single AI feature can be so much more expensive to run than the marketing suggested.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Reasoning models can solve harder problems, but every interaction still ends in words. What happens when a model can do more than answer?</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="horizon-curve" index={8}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">Third axis. Horizon.</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">The third generation is scaling on a different axis again. Not bigger networks, not longer thinking per query. The new dimension is how long the model can stay coherent on a single task without losing the thread.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">
    Five seconds. Five minutes. Five hours. A chat model can write a poem in a minute. A reasoning model can solve a contest math problem in twenty minutes. An agentic model can fix a bug in a real codebase, run the tests, and ship a pull request over the course of an afternoon. Capability scaled by <Term word="Horizon" definition="How long the model can stay coherent on a single task. Measured in seconds, minutes, or hours." analogy="A marathon runner's stamina. A sprinter wins a hundred meters. A marathoner runs for hours without losing form. The race is different.">horizon</Term> looks like a different kind of progress entirely.
  </p>
  <p class="animate-in">The benchmarks tell the same story. SWE-Bench, the standard test for autonomous software engineering, sat below fifteen percent with chat models. Reasoning models pushed it past thirty percent. Agentic models cleared seventy percent, almost entirely because they can iterate over long horizons without losing context. The model is not smarter than its predecessor. The work is just more complete.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Three axes, three eras. Each one shifts who pays. So what does it actually cost you?</p>
  <p class="animate-in scene-source">SWE-Bench Verified scores tracked through 2024-2025 across published reasoning and agentic models. The horizon framing is from METR's research on long-task completion.</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="cost-bar" index={9}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">Three generations, three different bills.</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">Training compute is the lab's problem. They pay it once, amortize it across every customer, and you never see it on your bill.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Inference compute is your problem. A reasoning model query can cost twenty or fifty times what the same prompt would have cost a chat model, because the thinking is real compute consumed at the moment you ask the question.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Horizon is the steepest of the three. An eight-hour agent run can cost ten dollars or more. The same prompt sent to a chat model would have cost a cent. Same input, different generation, hundred-fold cost difference.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">The trick is that each generation also unlocks tasks the previous one could not finish at all. So the comparison is not really "more expensive" in the way you might first read it. It is "expensive but possible" versus "cheap and impossible".</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Cost decides as much as capability. So how do you actually pick?</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="decision-tree" index={10}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">Pick the right model.</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">The fastest way to think about it is to ask what kind of work you are sending in.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">If you need a quick conversational answer, like a summary or a draft email or a one-line question, send it to a chat model. It will answer in a second, sometimes wrong, almost always cheap.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">If you need one good answer to a hard question, the kind that requires several steps of reasoning, send it to a reasoning model. It will cost more and take longer, but it will get math, logic, and complex code right where the chat model would have guessed.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">If you need work to actually finish in the world, with side effects, file edits, API calls, or multi-step tasks, send it to an agentic model. It is the only generation that can call tools and own the result.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Pick wrong in either direction and you suffer. The chat model gives you a wrong answer fast. The agentic model gives you the right answer but burns ten dollars to do what should have cost a cent. The match between the task and the generation is half the battle.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">In practice, you do not always get to pick. Model providers deprecate older versions and push customers up the generation ladder, sometimes with a few weeks of notice. The chat model that worked beautifully for your support bot last year may not be available this year. The skill is knowing what each kind of model is good for, so when a forced migration happens you can route the traffic that needs reasoning to a reasoning model and keep the simple cases on cheap chat without rebuilding the product every time the catalog changes.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Three generations. One concrete request. Now watch them all run.</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="three-models-extended" index={11}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">One task. Three answers. Three bills.</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">Plan and book a three-city trip next month under two thousand dollars. The same prompt, sent to all three generations, gives you three different stories.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">The chat model returns in five seconds. It lists three flights, two hotels, and a total of two thousand four hundred dollars. The math is wrong, the budget is blown, and nothing is booked. The query ran up about a cent.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">The reasoning model returns in thirty seconds. It worked through the constraints carefully. The math is right, the budget fits, and the itinerary is plausible. But it has not booked a single thing. The bill came to about fifty cents.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">The agentic model returns in four minutes. It checked real fares on real airline APIs, held seats, paid with a corporate card you authorized, and emailed you the confirmations. The trip is actually happening. The bill came to about four dollars.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Three different generations. Three different bills. Three different outcomes. None of them is the right answer in every situation. The right answer depends on what you actually needed in the first place.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">An agentic model that can do this much work needs tools. Someone has to build them.</p>
</Scene>
