import Scene from '../../components/Scene.astro';
import Term from '../../components/Term.astro';

<Scene animation="hero" index={0}>
  <p class="week-tag animate-in">Week 1</p>
  <h1 class="animate-in">Tell me a story.</h1>
  <p class="animate-in">You typed thirteen words. Claude wrote ninety four. It happened in three seconds.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Claude did not plan the plot. It did not know the ending before it started.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Here is what it actually did.</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="typewriter" index={1}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">The simple answer</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">Claude does not write a story. It predicts one word. Then another. Then another.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">That is all it ever does.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">So why does the story not fall apart after sentence two?</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="tokens" index={2}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">Tokens are not words</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">
    Before predicting anything, Claude breaks your sentence into <Term word="Tokens" definition="The smallest pieces a model reads. A token is roughly four characters or about three quarters of a word. The thirteen words in your prompt become eight tokens." analogy="The way you sound out syllables when you read an unfamiliar word out loud.">tokens</Term>.
  </p>
  <p class="animate-in">Eight tokens for thirteen words. Each one becomes a number.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">A number on its own means nothing. Unless you give it a location.</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="embeddings" index={3}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">Every token becomes a vector</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">
    Each token gets converted into a long list of numbers called an <Term word="Embedding" definition="A list of hundreds of numbers that represents one token. Tokens with similar meanings end up with similar number patterns. The embedding is a coordinate in a vast space of meaning." analogy="GPS coordinates for words. Door and gate live in the same neighborhood. Door and tuesday live in different cities.">embedding</Term>.
  </p>
  <p class="animate-in">Door and gate sit close together in this space. Door and tuesday sit far apart.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">The model has learned where every word lives. But location alone is not meaning. Tokens still have to talk to each other. How does the model make that happen?</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="neural-net" index={4}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">The neural network</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">
    Those vectors flow upward through a stack of layers called a <Term word="Neural Network" definition="A stack of mathematical layers. Each layer takes numbers in, multiplies them by stored weights, and passes new numbers to the next layer. The transformer is one specific kind of neural network." analogy="An assembly line. Raw materials enter at the bottom. Each station reshapes them. The finished product comes out the top.">neural network</Term>.
  </p>
  <p class="animate-in">Each layer reshapes the signal.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">By the top, the network has produced a probability for every possible next word in its vocabulary.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">One problem remains. A stack of layers reads tokens without knowing what surrounds them. "Bank" near "river" means something different than "bank" near "loan." Something has to let every token see every other token at the same time.</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="parallel" index={5}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">Tokens talk to each other</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">
    Inside each layer, every token looks at every other token. Each one asks the same question. How much do you matter for understanding me? This is called <Term word="Attention" definition="A mechanism that lets every token in the sequence look at every other token and assign each one a weight. The weights decide whose meaning gets blended into whose. Attention runs simultaneously, in parallel, for every token." analogy="Re-reading the start of a sentence when a word near the end surprises you.">attention</Term>.
  </p>
  <p class="animate-in">Some conversations are loud. Most are quiet.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Loud ones shape the next word more than quiet ones. But which ones? Every token scores every other. There is a specific calculation behind each connection.</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="heatmap" index={6}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">The story stays consistent</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">When Claude writes "she reached for the handle" eighty tokens later, it is still attending to door and closet from your original prompt.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">That is how the character stays the same person from the first sentence to the last.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Door at token four still affects door at token eighty four. But every new word Claude generates gets added to the list. How large does that list get before something has to give?</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="context-window" index={7}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">The context window grows</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">
    Every word Claude generates gets added to what it sees next time. By token ninety four, it is attending to the original prompt plus everything it has produced so far. This visible region is called the <Term word="Context Window" definition="Everything the model can see when it predicts the next token. It includes your prompt and all the text the model has generated so far. Modern models can hold hundreds of thousands of tokens in context." analogy="A whiteboard the model can read but not erase. Everything that goes on stays on, until the board fills up.">context window</Term>.
  </p>
  <p class="animate-in">Nothing disappears. Nothing is forgotten.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">But seeing something and understanding it are different things. What does the model actually do with ninety four tokens at once?</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="patterns" index={8}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">What the network learned</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">
    The model read millions of stories during training. The phrase "girl finds mysterious door" activates deep patterns about adventure and discovery. Those patterns live in billions of stored numbers called <Term word="Weights" definition="The numbers inside a neural network that decide how each layer transforms the signal. Training adjusts these numbers, billions at a time, until the network's predictions match what humans actually wrote." analogy="The settings on a giant audio mixer. Training is the long process of getting every dial in exactly the right position.">weights</Term>.
  </p>
  <p class="animate-in">The model does not think. It pattern matches.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">But if it always follows the most likely pattern, every story would be the same story. What stops that?</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="temperature" index={9}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">Temperature</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">
    A setting called <Term word="Temperature" definition="A number that controls how often the model picks an unlikely token instead of the top one. At zero, the model is fully deterministic. At one, the model surprises itself. At two, the model gets weird." analogy="The wildness dial on a creative writing assistant. Low for safe. High for strange.">temperature</Term> introduces controlled randomness.
  </p>
  <p class="animate-in">At zero, Claude always picks the top word. At seven tenths, it sometimes picks a less likely one. At one and a half, it gets strange.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Tokens. Attention. Patterns. Randomness. All four pieces on the table. Now watch them run ninety four times in a row.</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="pipeline" index={10}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">From one token to a paragraph</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">Now run the whole thing.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Prompt in. Tokens. Vectors. Layers. Attention. One output token. Add to context. Run it again.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Ninety four times. That is your paragraph.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">You typed thirteen words. Your words did more work than you think. The next question is what you can do with that knowledge.</p>
</Scene>

<Scene animation="prompt-comparison" index={11}>
  <h2 class="animate-in">What this means for your prompts</h2>
  <p class="animate-in">The more specific your prompt, the more anchors the model has to grip.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">"Write a story" gives Claude almost nothing. "Write a story about a girl who finds a door in her closet on her seventh birthday" gives it three strong keys it returns to throughout.</p>
  <p class="animate-in">Tell it who. Tell it where. Tell it when.</p>
</Scene>
